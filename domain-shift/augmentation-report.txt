	GAMMA CORRECITON

------------------------------------------------------

what is gamma:
	https://homepages.inf.ed.ac.uk/rbf/CVonline/LOCAL_COPIES/POYNTON2/GammaFAQ.html

Gamma correction models the nonlinear relationship between actual light intensity and pixel value. Camera sensors are roughly linear (double the photons, double the voltage) but displays and human vision are not. So manufacturers bake a gamma curve into the sensor firmware to compensate. Different cameras use different curves.

	 for each pixel value i in [0, 255], the new value is (i/255)^gamma * 255

So 0.45 means "gamma encoding applied but never decoded" and 2.2 means "gamma decoded but never encoded" — these are the two failure modes of a completely misconfigured pipeline end-to-end. They are extreme, almost worst-case scenarios. For a domain shift experiment on an industrial line, these are not realistic: no sane camera deployment would be that far off.

industrial cameras naturally have a gamma of 1, meaning they are linear in response by default. Displays can have a nonlinear response between 2 and 3, and the camera may optionally incorporate gamma correction to compensate when used together with a display -- edmundoptics

Industrial machine vision cameras are typically configured to output linear images. realistic domain shift scenarios are:

	someone accidentally enables the gamma correction setting on the camera (gamma goes from 1.0 to ~0.45 or ~0.5
	
	Different camera model from a different vendor ships with a slightly different tone curve baked in (gamma = 0.8 or 1.2)
	
	Camera firmware is updated and default gamma setting changes subtly

Per-channel diagonal scaling is the standard model for illuminant change

As a white light source's colour temperature rises in Kelvins, there is more blue content, resulting in a "cool" white. When the colour temperature is lower, there is more red light, perceived as a "warm" white. White LED lamps are produced by blue LEDs combined with a phosphor layer, and different phosphor chemistries produce different colour temperature profiles with varying balances of blue peak and red-orange content.
higher R → lower B (warm, low CCT) and higher B → lower R (cool, high CCT). G stays relatively stable because it sits in the middle of the spectrum and is less affected by the warm/cool axis — which is also why camera sensors are designed with the Bayer filter giving twice as many green pixels as red or blue.

going from a 3000K warm LED (common in older factory installations) to a 6000K cool LED (common in modern inspection lighting) is roughly a factor of 2 in the R/B ratio. Artificial light colour temperature is typically found in the range between 2700K and 6500K, with light appearing cooler and bluer as the Kelvin value increases.ange of 0.70–1.40 on R and B — giving a maximum R/B ratio of 2.0 — covers this full span of realistic industrial illuminants

	GAUSS NOISE / SALT-PEPPER

------------------------------------------------------


    Gaussian read-noise + optional salt-and-pepper dead/hot pixels.

    Gaussian sigma models thermal / read noise (low-light or high-gain).
    Salt-and-pepper fraction models sensor defects or transmission errors.

    Industrial case: industrial cameras operating at high gain
    (low-light) show significant read noise, and older sensors develop dead pixels.

The physical origin is the camera sensor itself. When light hits a photosite on a CCD or CMOS sensor, it generates electrons, this is the photoelectric effect. The count of electrons is then converted to a voltage and digitized to a pixel value. Two unavoidable noise sources contaminate this process at the hardware level:

	- Shot noise ==>	comes from the quantum nature of light itself. Even under perfectly stable illumination, 
		the number of photons arriving per unit time fluctuates according to a Poisson distribution. 
		At high light levels this is negligible (Poisson approaches Gaussian for large counts), but at low light it dominates

	- Read noise ==>	  (also called thermal noise or Johnson-Nyquist noise) comes from the random thermal motion of electrons 
			in the sensor circuitry during the analog-to-digital conversion step. It is well modelled as additive 
			zero-mean Gaussian noise, independent of the signal level. 

			It is well modelled as additive zero-mean Gaussian noise, independent of the signal level, like th efunction does
			Zero mean means the noise does not systematically bias the image brighter or darker, it adds random fluctuations symmetrically 
			above and below the true pixel value. Sigma controls the standard deviation , how wide the fluctuations are. 
			The higher the camera gain (ISO analog in industrial cameras), the larger sigma becomes,
			
			The additive model output = signal + noise is called the AWGN model (Additive White Gaussian Noise), it's a first-order model 
			for sensor noise, "White" here means the noise is spectrally flat so uncorrelated between pixels, with no spatial structure

			Range sigma [5, 40] 8bit px --> sigma = 5: SNR ca. 34 dB - good industrial cam, good light
						    --> sigma = 40: SNR ca. 16 dB - amera pushed to high gain in poor light, or a cheap sensor

	Poisson shot noise: more accurate than Gaussian for low photon counts,  requires careful scaling. 
		===  	The Gaussian approximation is adequate for 8-bit imagery at reasonable light levels 	===


============ Salt-and-pepper noise — modelling dead and hot pixels ===========

Individual photosites on a sensor can fail permanently :

		Dead pixels (pepper - black): 	the photosite produces zero output regardless of illumination, because the photodiode 
						is damaged or the readout circuit is broken. They appear as permanently black dots


		Hot pixels (salt — white): 		the photosite has excessive dark current, it generates electrons even with no light, saturating to maximum 
						value. They appear as permanently white dots.  Hot pixels worsen with sensor temperature and sensor age.

				hot pixels are actually a continuous spectrum, not binary, some pixels are just slightly elevated, not fully saturated. 					The binary model is a simplification


	range sp_frac ca. [0, 0.005] means between 0 and 0.5% of pixels are defective, which is realistic. Camera manufacturers typically specify 
	a maximum dead pixel count in their datasheets, often around 0.1–0.5% for industrial sensors.

In a real sensor both are always present simultaneously — you always have read noise (Gaussian, continuous) and potentially some dead pixels (salt-and-pepper, discrete). Separating them into two independent transforms would be less physically realistic. The combined function models "one sensor acquisition" as a single unit.

	BLUR

------------------------------------------------------

Motion blur occurs when there is relative movement between the camera and the object during the exposure window. 
The sensor integrates light over the entire exposure time, so if the object moves, each point in the scene 
traces a path across the sensor and the recorded pixel is the average of all positions along that path.

The mathematical model is a convolution. If the object moves in a straight line during exposure, the 
point spread function (PSF) (the blur kernel) is a line segment of length L pixels at angle θ. 
Every pixel in the output is the average of L input pixels along that direction
	output(x,y) = (1/L)  sum (input(x + t cosθ, y + t sinθ) )  for t  [0, L]

build a horizontal line of ones divided by L, then rotate it to angle θ using warpAffine

The two physical causes in your industrial context are distinct. 
	Conveyor vibration: the tapis vibrates at its mechanical resonance (typically 10–100 Hz). If exposure time is long relative to the 
	vibration period, the object traces a small arc, butlinear approximation holds for small amplitudes. 

	Object not frozen: if the part is moving along the belt and exposure is too slow to freeze it, you get directional blur 
	aligned with belt direction, predominantly horizontal. uniform angle  [0°, 180°] is generous, in practice belt motion blur clusters near horizontal

========== DEFOCUS BLUR =========

Parts with varying heights presented to a camera focused at nominal height will be defocused proportionally to their height deviation from the focal plane.

when the object is not at the focal plane. A lens focuses a point source at exactly one distance to a single point on the sensor. 
At any other distance, the point spreads into the circle of confusion.

the geometrically correct PSF is a flat disk, the pillbox function, however a Gaussian is the standard approximation
	sigma * 6 kernel size captures 3 standard deviations per side, containing 99.7% of the Gaussian energy which is correct and standard. 
	The | 1 forces odd size for a centred kernel

"Defocus blur was modelled using a Gaussian PSF, which is a standard approximation to the geometrically correct pillbox PSF and is accurate for the mild-to-moderate defocus levels considered here."

The point spread function (PSF) of a linear motion blur distortion is a line segment. Such a PSF is specified by two parameters: 
 length	 is the length of the blur and 
 theta 	is the angle of motion.

The blur in pixels is calculated as: B = Vp × Te × Np / FOV, where Vp is part velocity, Te is exposure time, Np is number of pixels spanning the field of view, and FOV is the field of view in the direction of motion. As an example, with Vp = 1 cm/sec, Te = 33ms, Np = 640 pixels and FOV = 10cm, the result is B = 2.1 pixels. In most cases, blurring becomes an issue past 1 pixel. ResearchGate
This formula is your anchor. Applying it to a realistic MVTec-scale scenario — say a 900px image covering 100mm, belt speed 200mm/s, exposure 5ms — gives B = 200 × 0.005 × 900 / 100 = 9 pixels. A typical conveyor belt inspection scenario: if the conveyor belt runs at 1 m/s and the camera's exposure time is 10ms, during a single exposure the object will move 10mm. This amount of displacement creates visible motion blur. ------------------------------------------------------

The mask is applied identically to all three BGR channels, which is physically correct, since natural vignetting is a geometric light-blocking effect, not a spectral one. It attenuates all wavelengths equally.

Natural vignetting -- the cos⁴ law
			fundamental and unavoidable in any lens system. The illuminance at a point on the sensor at angle θ from the optical axis falls as: E(θ) = E(0) × cos⁴(θ)
			At θ = 30° — typical for a moderate field-of-view machine vision lens -- cos⁴(30°) ≈ 0.56, meaning the corner receives only 56% of the centre illuminance. 
			That is already nearly 1 stop of falloff from physics alone, before any manufacturing or design factors.
			Gaussian mask approximates this cos⁴ profile. The cos⁴ law produces a slightly flatter centre and harder roll-off than a Gaussian, 
			but for simulation it's ok, visually indistinguishable
			I ONLY MODEL THIS ONE WITH THE GAUSSIAN

Optical vignetting -- The front lens elements partially eclipse the entrance pupil for oblique rays — off-axis beams see a smaller effective aperture than on-axis beams. 
			This is aperture-dependent: it reduces when you stop down, which is why machine vision lenses are often operated at f/5.6–f/8 to minimise it. 
			At large apertures (f/1.4–f/2) it can add another 0.5–1 stop on top of natural vignetting.

Mechanical vignetting -- The physical lens barrel, filter ring, or lens hood blocks the most oblique rays entirely, producing a harder-edged darkening.

	CLAHE - CONTRAST

------------------------------------------------------


The problem with global HE: it computes one transform for the whole image. If one region is dark and another is bright, the same transform is applied to both, which can severely over-enhance some regions and wash out others. It is also very sensitive to outliers
AHE fixes the global problem by dividing the image into tiles and computing a separate histogram equalization for each tile. Each pixel is enhanced based on the local statistics of its neighbourhood. 
The problem with AHE: in uniform regions (flat surfaces, backgrounds) the local histogram is very narrow and the equalization amplifies noise catastrophically.

CLAHE (Contrast‑Limited AHE)
CLAHE limits how much any local histogram bin can dominate:

	prevents extreme noise amplification
	keeps the enhancement realistic			 CLAHE = local contrast boost with an upper limit.

CLAHE adds one crucial modification: it clips the histogram before computing the CDF. Any histogram bin that exceeds clip_limit gets its excess redistributed uniformly across all other bins before equalization. This prevents any single intensity level from dominating the transform, which limits how aggressively low-contrast regions get enhanced.

CLAHE was originally developed for medical imaging and proven successful for enhancement of low-contrast images. It overcomes the limitations of standard histogram equalization by combining adaptive local processing with a contrast-limiting mechanism.

Zuiderveld, K.J. — "Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization", Graphics Gems IV, Academic Press, 1994, pp. 474–485.
https://www.cse.unr.edu/~bebis/CS474/StudentPaperPresentations/1994%20-%20CLAHE.pdf

	clip_limit
OpenCV's implementation defines it as the ratio of the clip threshold to the average bin count: 	clip_threshold = clip_limit × (tile_area / 256). 
clip_limit = 1.0 means clipping at the average,very aggressive, most bins are redistributed, the result approaches uniform equalization. 
clip_limit = 40.0 approaches plain AHE with minimal clipping. 
range of [1.0, 6.0] therefore spans from heavy clipping (subtle effect) to moderate clipping (stronger local enhancement with some noise amplification)

Industrial cameras implement AGC as automatic adjustment of sensor gain to achieve a preset brightness reference value, with a configurable gain limit that is not exceeded when AGC is enabled. The gain can be adjusted in mean mode, targeting the average brightness of the image, or in peak white mode, targeting a reference quantity of white pixels.

real camera AGC operates globally, it adjusts a single gain value for the whole sensor. But the perceptual effect of different AGC settings between two cameras is that images of the same scene will have different local contrast distributions, particularly in mixed-contrast scenes (dark background, bright object). 
CLAHE with varying clip limits reproduces this effect locally, different clip limits produce different local contrast enhancements that mimic what different AGC calibrations produce in practice. 
It is a functional simulation, not a physically exact one, but it is the standard approach in the augmentation literature for this type of domain shift.

	LAB (also written L*a*b* or CIELAB) is a colour space defined by the International Commission on Illumination (CIE) in 1976. It has three channels

	L: Lightness 	 perceived brightness, range [0, 100]. 0 is black, 100 is white.
	a: green		red opponent axis. Negative values are green, positive are red.
	b: blue		yellow opponent axis. Negative values are blue, positive are yellow.

Perceptual uniformity: 
equal numerical distances in LAB correspond to roughly equal perceived colour differences. This is not true in BGR or even HSV, a change of 10 units in blue looks very different from a change of 10 units in red perceptually

Luminance-chrominance separation: 
L contains all brightness information. a and b contain only colour information with no brightness component. We can process brightness independently of colour, change contrast without introducing any hue or saturation shifts.

https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12230580/		A systematic survey: role of deep learning-based image anomaly detection in industrial inspection contexts

Real cameras often have auto contrast enhancement
So the same object can produce different texture/contrast statistics without being defective.

This affects anomaly detectors because:
	contrast changes alter edge strength, fine scratches can be boosted or washed out, feature embeddings shift

clip_limit  [1.0, 6.0]
 Very large values will let the histogram equalization do whatever it wants, resulting in maximal local contrast. A value of 1 will result (almost, very subtle effects, barely distinguishable from the original) in the original image

Real AGC is a global operation, one gain value for the whole sensor. CLAHE is a local operation, different transforms for different regions. They produce different visual effects. CLAHE more accurately models a camera with spatially varying response (like pixel vignetting or fixed pattern noise that varies locally) than global AGC. 
a more precise framing is: "CLAHE with varying clip limits simulates spatially non-uniform contrast response variation between camera units, which in practice arises from differences in sensor calibration, AGC setpoint, and local fixed pattern noise." can't really go claiming it directly models AGC.

even with a fixture or vacuum gripper, parts have positional tolerances. A metal nut dropped onto an inspection tapis lands at a slightly different position and angle each time. The fixture constrains this but does not eliminate it.


	Camera mounting drift or remounting/replacement
cameras are bolted to frames that vibrate, thermally expand, and get bumped during maintenance. A camera that was calibrated at installation may be slightly translated or rotated relative to its original position weeks later.

Both effects mean the model sees the part at a slightly different position, orientation, and perspective than during training

	PERSPECTIVE

------------------------------------------------------

A camera viewing a flat object (the nut) from a slightly different angle does not just rotate or translate the image.
It creates perspective distortion: parallel lines can converge, and one side appears smaller.
That kind of distortion is called a projective transform (also called a homography).

A homography is a 3×3 matrix that maps a point (x,y) in one plane to a new point (x ′,y ′) in another plane, it's more general than an affine transform because it allows tilt and depth effects.

A homography is fully determined by 4 point correspondences.
So if you know where the four corners of the image should move, you can solve for the entire transform

Step 1: Define the camera intrinsic matrix K
pythonf = w
K = np.array([[f,  0, cx],
              [0,  f, cy],
              [0,  0,  1]], dtype=np.float64)
K is the camera intrinsic matrix, it encodes how a 3D point in camera coordinates gets projected onto the 2D image plane. It comes from the pinhole camera model:
	x_pixel = f × (X/Z) + cx
	y_pixel = f × (Y/Z) + cy
In matrix form this is exactly K. 

		the diagonal entries [f, f] are the focal length in pixels, they control how much the image magnifies the scene. 

		cx, cy is the principal point — where the optical axis intersects the sensor, assumed to be the image centre.

What K does in this context: it converts between pixel coordinates and normalised camera coordinates (rays from the optical centre). 
K_inv maps a pixel (u, v) to a unit ray direction. 
K maps a ray back to a pixel. 			!!!!  need both directions to build the homography.

Rx and Ry are the standard 3D rotation matrices around the x-axis (pitch) and y-axis (roll) respectively. They come directly from the definition of rotation in 3D Euclidean space.

	Rx rotates vectors around the x-axis (tilts the camera forward or backward), there a trapezoid effect that compresses the top edge

	Ry rotates vectors around the y-axis ( tilts the camera left or right) 
		ex. positive roll angle rotates the right side of the image away from the camera, compressing the right edge



	H = K @ R @ K_inv 

derives from the planar homography theorem
when a camera rotates (with no translation) around its optical centre while viewing a flat surface, the mapping between the original image and the new image is exactly a homography of this form.

Motivation
In computer vision, any two images of the same planar surface are related by a homography under the pinhole camera model. This relationship holds both when the scene is planar and when two cameras are related purely by a rotation around the optical centre. Industrial inspection systems are subject to both conditions: the inspected part (a metal nut) is approximately planar, and camera tilt after maintenance or imperfect mounting constitutes a pure rotation of the camera around its optical centre. This makes the homography the exact and correct mathematical model for the domain shift we want to simulate, rather than an approximation.

Why not random corner perturbation
The naive approach to perspective augmentation — independently jittering the four corners of the image and computing a homography from the perturbed quadrilateral — was rejected for a fundamental reason. A planar homography has eight degrees of freedom, and while four corner positions are widely adopted as a parameterisation, this approach lacks geometric interpretability. More critically, sampling those eight degrees of freedom randomly produces homographies that correspond to no physically realisable camera position

	SPECULAR REFLECTION

------------------------------------------------------

 creates a synthetic specular highlight,a bright glowing spot,and adds it onto the image using additive blending. The spot is modelled as a 2D Gaussian ellipse: maximum brightness at the centre (cx, cy), falling off smoothly toward zero at a distance determined by the radii rx and ry. Additive blending means pixel values only increase: the hotspot adds light, never removes it, which is physically correct for a reflection

The centre is constrained to the inner 60% of the image (0.2 to 0.8) to avoid hotspots entirely at the border where they would be half-cropped and look artificial. Radii are independently sampled for x and y, and this produces elliptical rather than circular hotspots which is physically correct since specular reflections aren't perfectly circular

	X, Y = np.meshgrid(np.arange(w), np.arange(h))
Builds two 2D arrays of the same shape as the image: X contains the column index at every pixel, Y contains the row index. This is the standard way to evaluate a function of position over an entire image without looping over pixels.


2D Gaussian evaluated at every pixel simultaneously. The formula is:

			G(x,y) = bright × exp( -( ((x-cx)/(rx/2))² + ((y-cy)/(ry/2))² ) / 2 )
			
				rx is not the standard deviation but twice it.
				At distance rx the hotspot still has 13.5% of peak intensity, because exp(-2) ca. 0.135

	hotspot = hotspot[:, :, np.newaxis]
newaxis makes hotspot broadcastable over the three BGR channels,the same intensity is added to B, G, and R equally, producing a white highlight.
 _clip prevents overflow above 255.


surfaces with Gaussian roughness distribution (the standard assumption in physically based rendering), this integral produces exactly a Gaussian-shaped highlight.
 This is the Torrance-Sparrow or Cook-Torrance microfacet model, where the Normal Distribution Function (NDF) governing the highlight shape is typically a Gaussian or GGX distribution. Gaussian hotspot it's the correct first-order model

Additive blending is correct because specular reflection adds energy to what the camera sees, it is light from the source reflected toward the camera on top of the diffuse surface appearance. Real HDR specular highlights saturate pixels to 255

A real specular highlight is stronger on surfaces that are already bright (high diffuse reflectance) and saturates there first. On dark surfaces the additive model is accurate. On already-bright surfaces (like the shiny face of a new metal nut), adding 200 brightness units to a pixel already at 180 just clips to 255, which is physically correct but produces a large flat saturated region that can look unnatural. A physically more accurate model would reduce the additive amount where the surface is already near saturation
